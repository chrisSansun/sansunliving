On Moving and What Follows You

May 2026 — Naomi Murray

Back to Journal

Most people who move abroad do a version of the same thing. They sort ruthlessly before they leave. They ship far less than they thought they would. They arrive lighter than they have been in years and, for a brief period, feel the extraordinary freedom of that.

And then, gradually, things accumulate.

This is not a failure of discipline. It is what happens when people build a life somewhere. Objects arrive as gifts, as practical necessities, as comfort during the difficult adjustment months. A new country brings new shopping habits, new solutions to new problems. Within a year, the lightness is gone.

Moving does not solve the underlying relationship with our possessions. It only pauses it.

I see this particularly often among expats in Sri Lanka, many of whom arrived with a clear vision of a simpler life and found that life does not simplify on its own. The climate, the household help, the different relationship with space and storage here, all of these require new systems. The ones from home rarely transfer.

What I offer in these situations is not a second sort. It is the chance to build something designed for the life you are actually living now, in this house, in this country. That is a different project, and usually a more interesting one.
