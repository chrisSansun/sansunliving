What I Do

What I Do

I help women create space — so the clarity, intelligence, and ability they already have can do their work.

Many of the women I work with have built successful careers, businesses, and families. They are capable, used to managing a great deal, and good at it. But there often comes a point when the space around them no longer reflects who they are now, or where they want to go next.

My work combines practical organisation with a more intuitive read of what is actually going on — in the home, and often beyond it. I look at what is no longer serving you, bring clarity to what has become unnecessarily complicated, and build systems that work for the life you are living today, not the one you were living when those systems were first set up.

Some women need perspective and direction. Others need structured, hands-on support to actually make the change and have it hold. Most need a bit of both. Every engagement is shaped around you, your home, and the chapter you are in.

The work itself might mean turning a room that has quietly become a source of frustration into one that feels calm and useful again. It might mean building systems that make family life run with less daily friction — fewer small decisions, less energy lost to things that shouldn't take energy. Sometimes it is about letting go of a version of yourself that no longer fits, so your home can reflect the life you are actually living now.

What follows is rarely just a tidier home. More often, it is clarity, capacity, and the sense of moving forward again.

The Work

How I Work

Every engagement begins with a conversation — honest, not rushed, and with no agenda other than understanding what is actually going on.

We start with what's bothering you most. Which rooms feel wrong, what the clutter is costing you, and whether the way you are living in your home still makes sense for the life you are actually in. You tell me what you know. I tend to notice the rest.

We talk about you, not just your home. What you are doing for yourself right now. What is working and what isn't. I won't add to your list — I'll help you find what is already there and make it work.

Then we look at what's possible. This is not about minimalism, and it is not about creating a home that photographs well for someone else's idea of order. It is about whether your space is genuinely working for you — not the you of five years ago, and not the you that you feel you ought to be by now. However your home looks, and however you choose to live in it, we work with that. Specific changes, made in the right order. You will feel the difference sooner than you expect.

We go as deep as the work requires. Some women need one room turned around — and that alone shifts something real. Others need a fuller reorganisation, clearing, restructuring, building systems that hold over time. We start where the weight is heaviest. We move at a pace that builds confidence. Everything is shaped around your life, the way you actually function, the way your days actually run. The aesthetics come later — when the foundation is ready, not before.

And then we build something that lasts. Not a system you'll abandon in a fortnight. Something that moves with you, reflects you, and supports you every day without demanding your constant attention. A home that holds you when you need stillness, and sends you out into the world when you are ready to share your sunshine.

What Becomes Possible

What Begins to Change

There often comes a point when a home that once worked well no longer feels as though it is supporting you in the same way. A room feels heavier than it should. Decisions are postponed. Things become harder to maintain. Nothing is dramatically wrong, yet something no longer feels quite right.

This work is not simply about creating order within your home. Organisation is the vehicle. What it carries is something larger — clarity, capacity, and room for what comes next.

A sense of ease returns. Not because everything is perfect, but because your home begins to support your life rather than compete with it. The constant mental load of managing your surroundings begins to lift. Your thinking becomes clearer. You feel calmer, more present, and less pulled in multiple directions.

Something else begins to move.

Not because everything externally is perfect, but because there is more clarity within. Time, energy and attention begin to return. Decisions feel easier. Possibilities become easier to recognise. What felt unnecessarily complicated begins to simplify.

When your environment begins to support you rather than compete with you, it becomes easier to hear yourself, trust yourself and imagine what comes next.

And it is often here that the next chapter begins.

The Woman Behind the Work

Naomi Murray

I have always known how to create order. As a child, my bedroom was my sanctuary — not out of obligation but out of instinct. A tidy room was an anchor, a place to think and breathe, a return to myself after a life that was, in every other direction, beautifully and wilfully expansive.

That instinct travelled with me through years of building a career across London and Sydney. I was capable and driven, and I moved through professional life with a confidence that served me well. What it did not do was leave much room for the person underneath — the one with ideas and creativity and a quiet but persistent sense that the grind was never going to be the whole story.

I had wanted out since I was twenty-one. It took me decades to leave.

What finally shifted was not one moment but an accumulation. Life got fuller. Motherhood arrived and expanded the world in every direction at once. The home that had always been my steadiest place began to feel like something I was managing rather than somewhere I lived. The organised mind that had served me so well was now simply holding everything together — and holding everything together, it turns out, is its own kind of exhaustion.

The move to Asia was the break for freedom I had been moving toward for years. In many ways it was exactly that. In others, it broke me open — beyond the surface of how I had been living and into something far more honest. Living beyond the familiar, beyond the systems and conveniences I had long taken for granted, showed me something I could not have learned any other way: that even the most organised mind is at the mercy of its surroundings. That environment is not the backdrop to a life. It is the condition for it.

That understanding became Sansun Living.

I live what I teach. On difficult days and easier ones, I have the freedom to swim, to write, to sit close to nature, to move through my own hours without asking permission. Not because everything is perfect — it is not — but because I make choices, again and again, that shape the life around me to support who I actually am.

That is what I want for you too.
